# How Much Does It Cost to Pressure Wash in Florida? (2026 Guide)

**Quick Answer:** Standard pressure washing in Florida costs between **$300 and $800** for most single-family homes, with the average homeowner paying around **$550**. The biggest factors driving your price are home size, surface condition, and the specific cleaning methods required for Florida’s humid climate.

Florida homes accumulate algae, mold, and salt buildup much faster than homes in other regions. This requires more aggressive cleaning techniques or specialized soft-wash solutions that cost more than basic pressure washing.

## Average Cost Breakdown

| Cost Level | Price Range | Typical Scope |
|-----------|------------|---------------|
| Budget | **$300 – $500** | House exterior, basic siding |
| Mid-Range | **$500 – $800** | House, driveway, walkways |
| High-End | **$800 – $1,500+** | Full soft-wash, roof, pool deck |

**Budget** covers a standard exterior wash on vinyl or stucco siding for a home under 2,000 square feet. It includes basic cleaning of accessible areas without specialized treatments.

**Mid-range** is the standard package for most Florida homeowners. It includes the house exterior plus concrete driveways and walkways. This handles the heavy algae and salt residue common in coastal areas.

**High-end** involves soft-washing the roof, cleaning pool decks, and treating for mold or mildew on stucco. This uses lower pressure and chemical solutions to protect surfaces while killing organic growth.

These prices reflect Florida market rates as of early 2026 and include labor, equipment, and water usage. They do not include stain removal or repairs if damage is found during the wash.

## What Affects the Cost in Florida

### 1. Square Footage of Home

Pressure washing pricing often correlates with the perimeter or square footage of the structure. A 1,500 sq ft ranch home costs less to wash than a 3,000 sq ft two-story home. Expect to pay **$0.15 – $0.30 per sq ft** for the exterior only. Two-story homes require ladders or lifts, adding **$100–$300** to the labor cost.

### 2. Surface Type and Material

Different materials require different cleaning methods. Vinyl siding is the easiest to clean. Stucco requires soft-washing to avoid damaging the texture. Brick and concrete can handle higher pressure but need sealants afterward. Soft-washing stucco costs **$200–$400** extra compared to standard siding.

### 3. Labor Rates in Florida

Professional labor in Florida runs **$60 – $90 per hour** depending on the region. South Florida markets like Miami and Fort Lauderdale are on the higher end due to demand and cost of living. North Florida rates are slightly lower but still above the national average.

### 4. Accessibility and Obstacles

A clear perimeter around the house makes the job faster. Overgrown landscaping, parked cars, or outdoor furniture adds time. Contractors often charge extra for moving heavy items or trimming vegetation to access the walls. Expect **$50–$150** for difficult access scenarios.

### 5. Water Discharge Regulations

Florida has strict rules about wastewater runoff containing oil or chemicals. You cannot pressure wash driveways or parking lots where contaminants flow into storm drains. Some contractors charge more to neutralize chemicals or contain runoff to comply with local environmental codes.

### 6. Organic Growth and Contamination

Florida humidity creates rapid mold and algae growth. A house with heavy black streaks or orange oxidation takes longer to clean than a recently washed home. Heavy contamination requires pre-treatment and dwell time, adding **$100–$250** to the service fee.

**House exterior** cleaning is the most common service. It removes dirt, pollen, and salt spray from siding. For stucco homes, soft-washing is required to prevent surface damage. This service keeps curb appeal high and protects the paint job.

**Driveway and walkways** accumulate oil stains, tire marks, and dirt. Concrete in Florida can develop efflorescence (white salt deposits) if not cleaned. High-pressure cleaning opens pores, so sealing afterwards is often recommended for longevity.

**Deck and patio** surfaces suffer from foot traffic and moisture. Wood decks need careful pressure settings to avoid splintering. Composite decking requires gentler methods. Cleaning these areas prevents slippery surfaces and extends the material life.

**Roof soft wash** is critical for Florida homes. High pressure damages shingles or tiles. Soft washing uses low pressure and biocides to kill algae without physical damage. This is essential for maintaining roof warranties and preventing premature aging.

**Pool deck** areas are prone to calcium buildup and algae. These surfaces are often exposed to pool chemicals and constant foot traffic. Regular cleaning prevents slipping hazards and keeps the pool area looking pristine for resale value.

## Florida-Specific Considerations

**Building Codes:** Florida Building Code requirements regarding wastewater runoff are strict. You cannot allow dirty water containing detergents or oils to enter storm drains. Professional contractors must contain and neutralize runoff. This adds time and equipment costs compared to regions with looser rules.

**HVHZ Requirements:** In Miami-Dade and Broward County, impact windows and doors are standard. High-pressure washing can damage the seals on these windows, leading to water intrusion. Contractors must use low-pressure techniques near impact-rated glazing to maintain the Florida Product Approval status.

**Permits:** Most Florida counties do not require a building permit for residential pressure washing. However, the contractor must hold a valid Florida Business Tax Receipt. Some municipalities require a water usage permit during drought conditions. Always verify local rules before scheduling.

**Insurance Impact:** A clean exterior improves curb appeal and can help during insurance inspections. Some carriers inspect properties after storms for mold risk. A clean home may reduce the likelihood of mold claims being denied due to lack of maintenance.

**HOA:** Many Florida communities have strict HOA rules about exterior cleaning. They often dictate when washing can occur (no weekends) and what chemicals can be used. Violating HOA rules can result in fines. Get written approval before starting work on the property.

**Season:** Best time to pressure wash is the dry season, November through April. Rainy season (May through October) brings daily afternoon thunderstorms. Scheduling during rain delays the drying process and increases the chance of mold returning immediately.

**My Safe Florida Home:** The program offers free wind inspections for eligible homeowners. If mold is found during an inspection, they may recommend remediation. While they don't pay for pressure washing directly, cleaning the exterior is a key step in maintaining the home’s wind mitigation status.

## How to Save Money on Pressure Washing in Florida

1.  **Bundle services together.** Cleaning the house, driveway, and pool deck in one day saves on travel time and setup costs. Contractors often offer **10–20% discounts** when bundling multiple exterior services into one appointment.

2.  **Schedule in the dry season.** November through April is peak demand for exterior maintenance. Booking early in the season or during the shoulder months (October or May) can secure lower rates before the rush begins.

3.  **Clear the perimeter yourself.** Move cars, patio furniture, and toys before the crew arrives. This saves labor time on-site. Some contractors will reduce the quote by **$50–$100** if you handle all prep work.

4.  **Choose soft-wash only when needed.** High pressure is faster but damages stucco. If you have vinyl siding, standard pressure washing is cheaper. Only pay for soft-wash if your contractor confirms your stucco or roof needs it.

5.  **Get multiple quotes.** Pricing varies by **30%** between contractors in the same city. Get at least three written estimates that detail the chemicals used, pressure levels, and cleanup procedures. Don't just pick the cheapest option.

## When to Pressure Wash — Warning Signs

**Visible black streaks on siding.** These are usually Gloeocapsa magma algae. They eat through paint and stucco if left untreated. In Florida, these streaks can appear within six months of the last wash.

**Green or orange growth on concrete.** This indicates mold or mineral deposits. It creates slippery surfaces on walkways and pool decks. Ignoring this can lead to liability issues if a guest slips and gets hurt.

**Paint peeling or bubbling.** Pressure washing can sometimes be the first step in a repainting project. If paint is failing, the dirt underneath is trapping moisture. Cleaning reveals the true condition of the surface before you commit to painting.

**Insurance or real estate inspection requests.** Some insurance companies require a clean exterior to approve policies. Real estate buyers often request a clean home before closing. A professional wash clears these hurdles quickly.

## How to Hire a Pressure Washing Contractor in Florida

1.  **Verify their Florida license.** While pressure washing doesn't always require a specific trade license, check for a General Contractor (CGC) or Registered Contractor (RC) if they do other work. Use [MyFloridaLicense.com](https://www.myfloridalicense.com) to verify status.

2.  **Confirm active insurance.** Ask for a Certificate of Insurance showing general liability (minimum **$1 million**) and workers comp. Call the insurer directly to verify the policy is active.

3.  **Check for a local business tax receipt.** This confirms they are registered locally, not a transient operator. Most Florida counties require this for any business operating within their jurisdiction.

4.  **Get 3–4 written estimates.** Each quote should itemize the service, chemicals used, pressure settings, and cleanup plan. Compare the scope of work, not just the bottom line.

5.  **Check reviews on Google and BBB.** Look for patterns in complaints, not just star ratings. Pay attention to comments about water damage or property damage during the wash.

6.  **Never pay more than 10% upfront.** Florida Statute 489.126 restricts contractor deposits. Payment should tie to milestones: deposit, wash complete, final inspection passed.

7.  **Get everything in writing.** Contract must include: scope, chemicals used, protection plan for plants, payment schedule, and warranty terms.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does pressure washing cost in Florida in 2026?

Most homeowners pay between **$300 and $800** for a standard package. The average is around **$550**. Your actual cost depends on home size, surface condition, and whether soft-washing is required for stucco or roofs.

### Do I need a permit to pressure wash in Florida?

No, you typically do not need a building permit for residential pressure washing. However, contractors must hold a valid business tax receipt. Some counties have water usage restrictions during droughts that may require a permit.

### How often should I pressure wash my Florida home?

You should wash the exterior **once a year**. Florida’s humidity and salt air cause rapid buildup. If you live near the coast, twice a year is recommended to prevent salt corrosion on siding and metal fixtures.

### Does pressure washing damage my roof?

High pressure can damage asphalt shingles and clay tiles. Soft-washing is the safe method for roofs in Florida. It uses low pressure and biocides to clean without physical damage. Always use a contractor experienced with soft-washing.

### Can I pressure wash my own home in Florida?

You can, but you need proper equipment. Consumer-grade washers often lack the pressure control needed for stucco. Improper use can force water behind siding, causing mold inside the walls. Professional results are safer and often cheaper when you factor in equipment rental.

### Does homeowners insurance cover pressure washing?

No, pressure washing is considered maintenance and is not covered by insurance. However, a clean exterior helps with claims. If mold is found after a storm, a clean home proves you maintained the property, which can help with claim approval.
