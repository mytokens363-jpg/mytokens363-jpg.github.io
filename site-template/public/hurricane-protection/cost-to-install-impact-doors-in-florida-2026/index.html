Cost To Install Impact Doors In Florida 2026

Updated April 5, 2026 10 min read

How Much Does It Cost to Install Impact Doors in Florida? (2026 Guide)

Quick Answer: Installing impact-resistant doors across a typical Florida home costs between $3,500 and $12,000 for a full set, with the average homeowner paying around $7,000. A single entry door typically runs $1,200 to $2,800 installed. The biggest factors driving your price are the number of openings, frame material choice, and Miami-Dade or Broward County compliance requirements.

Florida sits in one of the most active hurricane basins on earth, which means standard doors often fail during tropical storms. Impact-rated doors use laminated glass and reinforced frames to resist high winds and flying debris. This extra protection adds cost — but it also keeps your home insurable and safe during the storm season.

What Affects the Cost in Florida

1. Number of Openings

Most homeowners replace the main entry door plus sliding patio doors leading to the backyard. A single entry door costs $1,200–$2,800 installed. A sliding patio door runs $2,000–$4,500 installed. Replacing five to six doors across the property pushes the total to $8,000–$12,000.

2. Frame Material Choice

This is the single biggest price lever you control. Aluminum frames are the most affordable option at $800–$1,500 per door installed. Fiberglass hits the sweet spot for durability and insulation at $1,200–$2,200 per door installed. Steel is the premium choice for maximum security at $1,500–$3,000 per door installed.

3. Labor Rates in Florida

Labor rates for impact door installers in Florida run $75–$125 per hour, roughly 15–20% above the national average. Demand spikes every year after hurricane season as insurance companies push for upgrades. Specialized installers with HVHZ certification can charge another 10–15% premium over standard carpenters.

4. Permits and Inspections

Most Florida counties require a building permit for impact door replacement. Permit fees run $150–$500 depending on project value and county. The process includes a pre-installation review and final inspection. Your contractor should pull the permit — if they suggest skipping it, walk away.

5. Door Size and Customization

Standard 36-inch doors are cheaper to install than custom oversized entries. Adding sidelites or transoms above the door adds $500–$1,500 to the project. Custom sizes or non-standard configurations can add $1,000–$3,000 to the final bill.

6. HVHZ Code Requirements

Miami-Dade and Broward Counties have the strictest requirements in the state. Every door must carry a Miami-Dade NOA or Florida Product Approval. This limits material options and adds $500–$1,500 compared to non-HVHZ areas. Installation methods are stricter too — specific screw patterns and anchors are required.

Aluminum frames are the budget option. They are strong and resist corrosion but conduct heat. Best for coastal rentals or budget-conscious homeowners who don’t mind higher energy bills.

Vinyl frames hit the sweet spot for insulation and cost. Rated for 150 mph winds with proper install. This is what most South Florida homeowners pick for secondary doors.

Fiberglass is the premium choice for entryways. Rated to 180 mph and lasts 30–50 years. It mimics wood grain but resists rot and warping. Expect to pay $1,200–$2,200 per door.

Steel frames offer maximum security at 180 mph ratings with 30–50 year lifespans. Best for homeowners who want a “last door you’ll ever buy” solution. Higher upfront cost pays off over decades of security.

Wood clad is the aesthetic choice — 30+ year lifespan and 180 mph rating. Best for high-end neighborhoods where curb appeal matters most. Expect to pay $2,000–$4,000 per door.

Sliding patio doors are specific to backyard access points common in Florida homes. Different product category entirely from entry doors. They require reinforced tracks and multi-point locks.

Florida-Specific Considerations

Building Codes: Florida Building Code 8th Edition (2023) governs all door replacements statewide. Key requirements include specific wind load ratings, impact testing for glass, and reinforced anchoring to the header and sill. These go beyond what most states require for residential properties.

Hurricane Zone: Miami-Dade and Broward Counties are in the HVHZ — the strictest wind zone in Florida. All door materials must hold a Miami-Dade NOA. Products are tested with missile impact tests and cyclic pressure tests that simulate hurricane conditions. Your contractor should provide NOA numbers for every product they plan to use.

Permits: Apply through your county’s online portal or building department. Miami-Dade uses ePlan, while Broward uses a similar digital system. Turnaround is 5–10 business days for a standard residential permit. Budget $150–$500 for the fee. One inspection required after installation.

Insurance Impact: This is the big one. A new impact door resets your wind mitigation profile. Many Florida insurers offer discounts for impact-resistant openings. Upgrading from standard doors to impact doors can qualify you for premium reductions of 10–30% on your windstorm portion.

HOA: Many Florida communities restrict door color, style, and hardware. Wood finishes are often required in deed-restricted communities. Get written HOA approval before signing a contractor agreement — changing materials mid-job is extremely expensive.

Season: Best time to install doors here is December through April — dry season, lower humidity, fewer storm damage backlogs. Avoid peak hurricane season (August–October). Rainy season (May–October) brings daily afternoon thunderstorms that delay projects.

My Safe Florida Home: If your home was built before 2008 and is homesteaded, you may qualify for a free wind inspection and matching grants up to $10,000 toward hurricane hardening improvements including door upgrades.

How to Save Money on Impact Door Installation in Florida

  1. Get at least 4 written quotes. Contractor pricing varies by 20–35% for identical scope in Florida. On a $7,000 job, that’s $1,500–$2,500 in potential savings. Make sure each quote itemizes materials, labor, removal, permits, and disposal separately.

  2. Schedule in dry season. December through April is slower for door installers. You’ll have more leverage to negotiate and fewer weather delays. Some contractors offer 5–10% off-season discounts.

  3. Choose fiberglass over steel if your HOA allows it. The savings between steel ($1,500–$3,000/door) and fiberglass ($1,200–$2,200/door) can be $1,500–$3,000 on a typical home. Fiberglass looks nearly identical but costs less.

  4. Bundle hurricane upgrades with the roof. Installing impact windows or doors during a roof replacement is 40–60% cheaper than standalone projects. The combined wind mitigation improvements can also cut your insurance premium by hundreds per year.

  5. Check for My Safe Florida Home eligibility. If your home qualifies, you can get up to $10,000 in matching grants for impact doors. This effectively cuts your out-of-pocket cost by half or more on qualifying properties.

When to Install Impact Doors — Warning Signs

Your insurance company is pressuring you. A non-renewal notice or letter requesting impact upgrades means the clock is ticking. Many Florida insurers are actively dropping homes with standard glass doors in coastal zones.

Visible cracks or delamination in glass. Check your current glass for fogging or cracks. Small chips can spread quickly during high winds. Compromised glass offers zero protection against hurricane debris.

Drafts and energy inefficiency. If you feel air coming through the frame, your seals have failed. In Florida’s humidity, this leads to mold and rot behind the frame. Poor seals also spike your cooling bills significantly.

Your home is over 20 years old. Standard doors installed in the 2000s often lack modern impact ratings. A 20-year-old door in Miami has taken more abuse than a 10-year-old door in the Midwest.

How to Hire a Door Contractor in Florida

  1. Verify their Florida license at MyFloridaLicense.com. You need a Certified General Contractor (CGC) or Certified Pool Contractor (CPC) license.

  2. Confirm active insurance. Ask for a Certificate of Insurance showing general liability (minimum $1 million) and workers comp. Call the insurer directly to verify.

  3. Check for a local business tax receipt. This confirms they’re registered locally, not a storm chaser from out of state.

  4. Get 3–4 written estimates with line-item breakdowns — material brand, glass type, frame material, tear-off, permits, labor, timeline, and warranty.

  5. Check reviews on Google and BBB. Look for patterns in complaints, not just star ratings.

  6. Never pay more than 10% upfront. Florida Statute 489.126 restricts contractor deposits. Payment should tie to milestones: deposit, delivery, mid-project, final inspection passed.

  7. Get everything in writing. Contract must include: scope, material specs with NOA numbers, dates, payment schedule, warranty terms, permit responsibility, and cleanup plan.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to install impact doors in Florida in 2026?

Most homeowners pay between $3,500 and $12,000 for a full set. The average is around $7,000. Your actual cost depends on the number of doors, frame material, and whether structural repairs are needed.

Do I need a permit to install impact doors in Florida?

Yes, always. Apply through your county’s building department portal. Budget $150–$500. One inspection required. Never let a contractor skip the permit — it can void your insurance and create title issues when you sell.

How long does a door installation take in Florida?

Single door replacements take 4–8 hours for an average home. Full sets take 1–2 days. Rainy season can add 2–5 days of weather delays.

Does installing impact doors increase home value in Florida?

Impact doors recoup 60–70% of their cost at resale. But the bigger value in Florida’s market is insurability — a home with impact doors is dramatically easier to sell because buyers can actually get insurance on it.

Can I install impact doors myself in Florida?

Florida’s homeowner exemption allows it if you own and occupy the home. But you still need permits, inspections, and must meet all HVHZ code requirements. Insurance companies may refuse to cover a homeowner-installed door. For most people, it’s not worth the risk.

Does homeowners insurance cover impact door replacement?

Storm damage (wind, hail, fallen trees) is typically covered minus your deductible — often 2% of insured value for hurricane claims in Florida. Normal wear and aging is never covered.

Bottom Line

Installing impact doors in Florida runs $3,500 to $12,000 for most homes, with fiberglass at the sweet spot of cost and performance. The most important thing to know: Florida’s HVHZ requirements add cost but your new doors meet some of the toughest wind standards in the country — and it keeps you insurable in a market where carriers are dropping homes with aging glass every day.
