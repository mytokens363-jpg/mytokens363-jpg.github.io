Cost To Install Hurricane Shutters In Miami 2026

Updated April 5, 2026 9 min read

How Much Does It Cost to Install Hurricane Shutters in Miami? (2026 Guide)

Quick Answer: A full set of hurricane shutters in Miami costs between $4,500 and $12,000 for a typical single-family home, with the average homeowner paying around $7,500. The biggest factors driving your price are shutter type, window count, and Miami-Dade County’s strict building code requirements.

Miami sits inside the High Velocity Hurricane Zone, which means every shutter must hold a Miami-Dade Notice of Acceptance. This ensures your protection meets the toughest wind standards in the country.

These prices reflect Miami market rates as of early 2026 and include materials, labor, permits, and cleanup. They do not include structural reinforcement if your existing window frames need strengthening before installation.

What Affects the Cost in Miami

1. Shutter Type

This is the single biggest price lever you control. Colonial shutters are the cheapest option at $150–$300 per window installed. Accordion shutters are the most popular for convenience at $250–$450 per window. Motorized roll-down units are the premium choice at $500–$900 per window but offer the highest convenience factor.

2. Number of Windows

Miami homes vary significantly in size. A small bungalow might have 10 windows requiring protection, while a larger Mediterranean home could have 30 or more. If you average $350 per window, a 10-window home runs roughly $3,500, while a 30-window home pushes $10,500.

3. Installation Complexity

Installing shutters on standard rectangular windows is straightforward. Installations on curved windows, arches, or sliding glass doors cost more due to custom framing. Complex installations can add $1,000–$3,000 to the project total depending on the number of unique shapes.

4. Permits and Inspections

Miami-Dade County requires a building permit for most shutter installations. Permit fees run $150–$400 depending on project value and location. The process includes submitting engineering drawings and a final inspection to ensure compliance with HVHZ codes.

5. Labor Rates in Miami

Installation labor in Miami-Dade runs $60–$90 per hour, roughly 20% above the national average. Demand peaks before hurricane season starts in May. Contractors often charge a premium for emergency installations or work scheduled during the rainy season.

6. HVHZ Code Requirements

Miami-Dade is in the High Velocity Hurricane Zone. Every shutter product must carry a Miami-Dade NOA. This limits material options and adds $1,000–$2,500 compared to non-HVHZ areas. Fastener schedules are stricter too — you cannot use standard drywall anchors in masonry walls.

Colonial shutters are the budget option. They are decorative and functional but require manual storage and deployment. Best for historic districts where aesthetics matter more than convenience.

Aluminum accordion shutters hit the sweet spot — rated to 180 mph wind, 20-year lifespan, and the best cost-to-value ratio. This is what most Miami homeowners pick for full coverage.

Aluminum roll-down is the premium convenience choice. Rated to 180 mph and 20-year lifespan, they operate with a crank or motor. Expect to pay $25.00–$45.00 per sq ft for the system and track.

Polycarbonate panels offer clarity while protecting against impact. Rated to 150 mph and last 15–20 years but cost roughly double aluminum. Your window frame also needs to handle the extra weight.

Steel rolling shutters offer maximum durability at 30+ years with 180 mph wind resistance. Best for homeowners who want a permanent, heavy-duty solution. Higher upfront cost pays off over decades.

Impact glass is specific to window replacement projects rather than add-on shutters. Different product category entirely from exterior shutter systems but provides similar protection without the need for deployment.

Florida-Specific Considerations

Building Codes: Florida Building Code 8th Edition (2023) governs all shutter installations in Miami. Key requirements include secondary water barrier on the entire roof deck, specific nail patterns for shingle attachment, and enhanced flashing at all penetrations. These go beyond what most states require.

Hurricane Zone: Miami is in the HVHZ — the strictest wind zone in Florida. All shuttering materials must hold a Miami-Dade NOA. Products are tested with missile impact tests and cyclic pressure tests that simulate hurricane conditions. Your contractor should provide NOA numbers for every product they plan to use.

Permits: Apply through Miami-Dade County’s ePlan online portal or at the Miami-Dade Building Department. Turnaround is 10–15 business days for a standard residential permit. Budget $150–$400 for the fee. Two inspections required: pre-install and final.

Insurance Impact: This is the big one. A new roof resets the clock with your insurance company. Many Florida insurers won’t write or renew policies on homes with roofs older than 15 years — some draw the line at 10. Upgrading to a hip roof shape or adding hurricane straps during replacement can qualify you for wind mitigation discounts of 15–45% on your premium.

HOA: Many Miami communities restrict shutter color, style, and operation method. Colonial shutters are often required in deed-restricted communities. Get written HOA approval before signing a contractor agreement — changing materials mid-job is extremely expensive.

Season: Best time to install shutters here is November through March — dry season, lower humidity, fewer storm damage backlogs. Avoid peak hurricane season (August–October). Rainy season (May–October) brings daily afternoon thunderstorms that delay projects.

My Safe Florida Home: If your home was built before 2008 and is homesteaded, you may qualify for a free wind inspection and matching grants up to $10,000 toward hurricane hardening improvements including shutter upgrades.

How to Save Money on Hurricane Shutters in Miami

  1. Get at least 4 written quotes. Contractor pricing varies by 20–35% for identical scope in Miami. On a $7,500 job, that’s $1,500–$2,500 in potential savings. Make sure each quote itemizes materials, labor, permits, and disposal separately.

  2. Schedule in dry season. November through March is slower for shutter installers. You’ll have more leverage to negotiate and fewer weather delays. Some contractors offer 5–10% off-season discounts.

  3. Choose accordion shutters over roll-down if your budget allows. The savings between accordion ($250–$450/window) and motorized roll-down ($500–$900/window) can be $3,000–$6,000 on a typical home.

  4. Bundle shutter upgrades with the roof. Adding roof-to-wall straps or upgraded underlayment during a replacement is 40–60% cheaper than standalone projects. The combined wind mitigation improvements can also cut your insurance premium by hundreds per year.

  5. Check for insurance claim eligibility. If your existing shutters have storm damage, your insurance may cover part or all of the replacement. File the claim before signing a contractor agreement. Be cautious of contractors who offer to “handle the insurance” — work with your adjuster directly.

When to Install Hurricane Shutters — Warning Signs

Your insurance company is pressuring you. A non-renewal notice or letter requesting a wind mitigation inspection means the clock is ticking. Many Florida insurers are actively dropping homes without adequate protection.

You are in a coastal flood zone. Miami-Dade County has specific flood zones that require higher elevation and stronger protection. Standard shutters may not meet flood elevation requirements if your windows are too low.

Your current shutters are 15+ years old. Aluminum oxidizes and polycarbonate yellows over time. A 15-year-old shutter might look fine but could fail the missile impact test in a Category 3 storm.

You plan to sell your home. Buyers in Miami expect hurricane protection. Installing shutters now can increase your home’s marketability and potentially reduce your buyer’s insurance premiums.

You have sliding glass doors. These are the most vulnerable entry points for wind-driven rain. Standard shutters often miss these openings, leaving your interior exposed to water intrusion.

How to Hire a Hurricane Shutter Contractor in Miami

  1. Verify their Florida license at MyFloridaLicense.com. You need a Certified Roofing Contractor (CCC), Registered Roofing Contractor (RC), or General Contractor (CGC) license.

  2. Confirm active insurance. Ask for a Certificate of Insurance showing general liability (minimum $1 million) and workers comp. Call the insurer directly to verify.

  3. Check for a Miami-Dade County local business tax receipt. This confirms they’re registered locally, not a storm chaser from out of state.

  4. Get 3–4 written estimates with line-item breakdowns — material brand, shutter type, number of windows, tear-off, permits, labor, timeline, and warranty.

  5. Check reviews on Google and BBB. Look for patterns in complaints, not just star ratings.

  6. Never pay more than 10% upfront. Florida Statute 489.126 restricts contractor deposits. Payment should tie to milestones: deposit, mid-project, final inspection passed.

  7. Get everything in writing. Contract must include: scope, material specs with NOA numbers, dates, payment schedule, warranty terms, permit responsibility, and cleanup plan.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a shutter installation cost in Miami in 2026?

Most homeowners pay between $4,500 and $12,000. The average is around $7,500. Your actual cost depends on shutter type, window count, complexity, and whether structural repairs are needed.

Do I need a permit to install shutters in Miami-Dade County?

Yes, always. Apply through Miami-Dade County’s ePlan portal. Budget $150–$400. Two inspections required. Never let a contractor skip the permit — it can void your insurance and create title issues when you sell.

How long does a shutter installation take in Miami?

Accordion shutters take 1–2 days for an average home. Roll-down shutters take 3–5 days. Rainy season can add 2–5 days of weather delays.

Does a new shutter increase home value in Miami?

Shutters recoup 60–70% of their cost at resale. But the bigger value in Miami’s market is insurability — a home with shutters is dramatically easier to sell because buyers can actually get insurance on it.

Can I install shutters myself in Florida?

Florida’s homeowner exemption allows it if you own and occupy the home. But you still need permits, inspections, and must meet all HVHZ code requirements. Insurance companies may refuse to cover a homeowner-installed shutter. For most people, it’s not worth the risk.

Does homeowners insurance cover shutter replacement?

Storm damage (wind, hail, fallen trees) is typically covered minus your deductible — often 2% of insured value for hurricane claims in Florida. Normal wear and aging is never covered.

Bottom Line

Installing hurricane shutters in Miami runs $4,500 to $12,000 for most homes, with accordion shutters at the sweet spot of cost and performance. The most important thing to know: Miami’s HVHZ requirements add cost but your new shutters meet some of the toughest wind standards in the country — and they keep you insurable in a market where carriers are dropping homes with aging roofs every day.
